The Effects of Covid in Europe

COVID-19 disrupted education, economies, and healthcare systems across Europe, leaving long-term effects on society. Schools shifted to remote learning, businesses faced economic instability, and healthcare systems struggled under unprecedented pressure. This project analyzes key data on infection rates, economic shifts, and healthcare responses to reveal how the pandemic reshaped daily life and societal structures across the continent.

Covid Spread

Throughout the COVID-19 pandemic, widespread attention has highlighted the profound impact of the virus. In this section, we explore how infections, deaths, and vaccination rates evolved over time, offering a clear view of when each metric peaked and when the situation eventually stabilized.

Cases, Deaths and Vaccines in countries during COVID-19

After the initial few cases, the situation rapidly deteriorated, especially following the emergence of the Omicron variant in November 2021. However, thanks to widespread vaccination efforts, the severity of the pandemic gradually diminished, paving the way toward recovery.

Economy

The COVID-19 pandemic and lockdown had a profound economic impact on Europe, causing changes in various sectors such as the housing market, GDP, and tourism. In this section, we will explore the impact of the pandemic, analyzing whether it slowed down the market in different aspects or, on the contrary, fueled new dynamics.

Flow of GDP and House Price during Covid years

The plot shows the trend of house prices and the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) in the various quarters from 2020 to 2023, maintaining the relationship with the number of new cases in the analyzed country. The GDP indicates the percentage growth rate of real GDP, adjusted for seasonal fluctuations and calendar irregularities. It tells you whether the economy is growing or contracting compared to the previous quarter, by comparing consecutive periods without distortions due to seasonality or calendar variations, as the data has been adjusted for both seasonal effects (variations due to holidays, seasonal cycles) and calendar differences (e.g., leap years). If the value is positive, it means the economy has grown compared to the previous period, while if it is negative, it means there has been an economic contraction. Meanwhile, the house price provides an overall overview of the real estate price trends, showing the percentage change in house prices compared to the previous quarter.

GDP has experienced more pronounced fluctuations compared to house prices, showing greater sensitivity to economic shocks. The data indicates that the impact of the pandemic was most evident in the early quarters of 2020, while in the following years, a gradual normalization of the economy was observed. Due to the pandemic, GDP saw a sharp decline, especially in the first two quarters of 2020, with negative values for almost all European countries. In Italy, where the country was under lockdown from March 9, 2020, to May 18, 2020, GDP dropped by -5.8% in the first quarter (Q1) and by -12.3% in the second quarter (Q2). After the reopening in the third quarter (Q3), GDP rebounded significantly in many countries (Italy +14.4%, Spain +15.9%, France +15.6%). In general, for most countries, from 2021 onwards, a gradual recovery was observed without extreme peaks until 2023. Regarding house prices, a different trend can be observed, with greater resilience compared to GDP. In Italy, for example, in the second quarter (Q2) of 2020, there was an increase of +3%, followed by a slight decrease of -2.5% in the third quarter (Q3). In the 2021-2023 period, the situation varies across different countries: in Italy, moderate fluctuations between positive and negative values were recorded, while in other countries, such as Luxembourg, a continuous decline in prices has been observed since the last quarter of 2023. Finally, the data shows that the months with the highest number of new COVID-19 cases were at the end of 2021 and the beginning of 2022. However, likely thanks to the widespread distribution of vaccines, these peaks did not lead to prolonged lockdowns or significant declines in GDP and house prices, indicating a greater ability of the economy to adapt compared to the early stages of the pandemic.

Inbound tourism during Covid

The following plot presents data on inbound tourists, represented by the size of the flag, in relation to the total number of infected cases (x-axis), expressed as a percentage of the population, and the number of vaccinated individuals(y-axis), also expressed as a percentage of the population. The data refers to the number of vaccinated and infected individuals in that specific year.

From the plot, it can be observed that from 2020 to 2022, there was a significant increase in infections. For instance, countries like France saw a rise from 3.53% in 2020 to 9.61% in 2021, reaching 44.18% in 2022, which represents a growth of 40.65% from 2020 to 2022. Similar trends are visible in countries such as Germany and Italy. On the other hand, different trends can be observed in countries like Poland, where the infection rate increased from 3.38% in 2020 to 7.33% in 2021, followed by a decrease of 1.44% in 2022 (infection rate in 2022: 5.89%). In 2021, most countries recorded a high vaccination rate. For example, Italy (81.26%), France (80.31%), Portugal (90.16%), and Spain (82.76%) reached very high levels. However, in 2022, the percentage of newly vaccinated individuals dropped drastically, with values around 2-4% in most countries. Regarding inbound tourism, what can be observed is that despite the surge in infections in 2022, tourism increased in countries like France, Italy, and Germany, as well as in most other countries. This was likely facilitated by the high vaccination rates achieved in 2021, which encouraged the return of tourists. Moreover, it highlights a global shift in attitudes towards the virus. However, some countries, such as Norway, the Netherlands, and Slovenia, deviated from the general trend, experiencing stagnant or declining tourism in 2022 despite a pandemic situation similar to other countries. This suggests that additional factors, such as local restrictions or economic conditions, may have played a role.

Education & Jobs

The COVID-19 pandemic has reshaped the landscape of education and employment in profound ways. In this section, we explore the challenges and adaptations within higher education and the job market. From the rapid shift to remote learning and digital classrooms to the evolving nature of work and employment opportunities, we delve into how these changes have impacted students, educators, and professionals alike. Join us as we examine the trends, impacts, and future possibilities in education and jobs during and after the pandemic.

Lockdown Impact on Education (2019-2021)

Another assumption we had was that prolonged lockdowns would have significantly impacted tertiary enrollment numbers. We anticipated that regions with extended closures would show a negative trend in enrollment growth. However, the data above indicates that among the surveyed countries, there has been little to no decline in the percentage of tertiary enrollments. This unexpected result suggests that rapid adaptations, such as the shift to online learning and the implementation of innovative teaching methods, may have helped mitigate the potential negative effects of extended lockdown periods. Further analysis could explore whether these adaptive strategies have led to long-term improvements in the resilience of higher education systems.

COVID-19 impact on Education (Males, Females and Total)

Our first assumption was that COVID-19 influenced, either positively or negatively, tertiary enrollments, primarily due to the abrupt shift to remote learning and the economic uncertainty that accompanied the pandemic. A further analysis indicates that the overall long-term trend in enrollments is stable and shows only minor deviations from historical patterns. In some countries, educational enrollment trends changed after COVID-19 - such as in Czechia - although overall, the trends remained stable. Moreover, across Europe, females tend to enroll in higher education more frequently than males. However, exceptions exist; for example, in Germany, Greece and Turkey on top, the difference is minimal. Specifically, in Turkey the gap shrinked afer the pandemic, while in some cases, such as in Cyprus, the gap between male and female enrollments has widened.

COVID-19 impact on Unemployment (Males, Females and Total)

During the early days of the pandemic, nearly every European country saw a sudden increase in unemployment as lockdowns forced many businesses to close or reduce operations. This quick rise was particularly noticeable in industries like hospitality, tourism, and retail, where job losses happened almost overnight. Over time, however, government support, financial aid, and changes in business strategies helped the job market stabilize, and by 2023, unemployment rates had started to return to their pre-pandemic levels.

Across Europe, the unemployment picture is a bit more varied. Generally, men experienced slightly higher unemployment rates than women, but this was not the case everywhere. For example, in Croatia and Cyprus, there were periods when either men or women were more affected by job losses. In Germany, the gap is more evident, with men facing significantly higher unemployment than women, while in Greece and Portugal, women tended to have higher unemployment rates at times. These differences highlight the varied economic and social factors that have shaped the labor market during and after the pandemic.

Mental disorders & Healthcare

This section explores the impact of the pandemic on mortality rates related to mental disorders and self-harm, shedding light on the potential consequences of prolonged social isolation, economic distress, and healthcare disruptions. Additionally, it examines the relationship between healthcare spending, total cases, vaccinations, and COVID-19-related deaths. Understanding these connections provides insight into how different levels of investment in healthcare may have influenced pandemic outcomes and the broader implications for public health policies.

Did the pandemic affect the number of deaths for mental disorders?

Select the causes of deaths

In most countries, the percentage of deaths attributed to mental and behavioral disorders has remained relatively stable over the years, with a slight increase in some cases. However, the actual number of deaths from these causes has risen in many countries. In Germany and Italy, for example, deaths due to mental and behavioral disorders increased by 20.4% and 9%, respectively - rising from 60,016 in 2019 to 72,243 in 2022 in Germany and from 27,673 to 30,145 in Italy over the same period.

The same trend applies to deaths caused by intentional self-harm, with Germany again showing one of the highest increases: 12.3%, from 9,104 in 2019 to 10,224 in 2022.

As for COVID-19, the number of deaths has decreased in most countries, peaking in 2021. On average, it accounted for 10% of total deaths that year, though in some countries, such as Slovakia and Czechia, the rate nearly reached 20%.

COVID Deepened the Mental Health Crisis: Visualizing Deaths by Country

Let's now examine how the average annual number of deaths from mental and behavioral disorders has changed before and after the pandemic in the 10 countries reporting the highest death toll. We will also explore the distribution of these deaths across different age groups and between genders.

It's clear we were right about Germany: the average annual deaths from mental and behavioral disorders increased by 17.9%, peaking at an average of 65,854 deaths between 2020 and 2023. Despite this significant number, Germany ranks as the country with the second-largest percentage increase; the most alarming record belongs to Portugal, which saw an increase of nearly 38%. Italy is the only other country that reported an increase in average annual deaths caused by mental and behavioral disorders, rising by 11.8%.

Regarding gender, the data indicates that the male-to-female death ratio for mental and behavioral disorders has remained largely unchanged before and after COVID-19. However, the same cannot be said for age groups: in all countries, the majority of these deaths continue to be concentrated in the 65+ age group, while the percentage of deaths among those aged 15-64 has more than doubled across all countries. The highest increases were observed in Germany (rising from 0.26% to 8.35%) and The Netherlands (rising from 0.36% to 7.21%).

While we cannot be 100% certain that COVID-19 had a direct impact on this phenomenon, as other factors may also be involved and would require further analysis, we can confidently say that it likely influenced these trends.

Does Increased Healthcare Spending Reduce Mental Disorders Deaths?

With the COVID-19 outbreak, health spending increased significantly, supported in many countries by additional EU funding. Given this rise in expenditures, let's explore whether there is a relationship between each country's health spending and the percentage of deaths attributed to mental and behavioral disorders over time.

We observe that prior to the COVID-19 pandemic, there appeared to be a slight positive correlation between health expenditure per capita and the percentage of deaths attributed to mental and behavioral disorders. This suggests that countries with higher health spending also reported higher percentages of such deaths, while those with lower spending had lower percentages. However, post-pandemic data indicates a slight disruption in this pattern, leading to a more scattered distribution with no clear correlation. This change may be attributed to various factors, including the pandemic's impact on mental health services, shifts in healthcare priorities, and the overall strain on healthcare systems. It's important to note that the relationship between health expenditure and mental health outcomes is complex and influenced by numerous variables. Further research is necessary to understand the underlying causes of these observations and to determine the specific impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on this relationship.

Health spending during COVID-19

This data appears disproportionate when compared with the number of infections, which in Italy represented 64.53% of total cases, compared to other countries such as Germany, where the percentage of infections was 95.46% in 2022. This comparison highlights how Italy has recorded a particularly high impact in terms of mortality compared to the expenditure incurred and the number of infections, suggesting possible differences in the effectiveness of health measures, in the organization of the hospital system or in the timing of intervention compared to other European countries.
